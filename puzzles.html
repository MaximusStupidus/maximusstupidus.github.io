1. Self-avoiding paths on a grid

A token sits at the origin of an infinite square lattice. In one step it may move one unit up, down, or right — never left. The walk must be self-avoiding: the token may never occupy a lattice point it has already visited.

For a walk of exactly N steps, how many distinct paths are possible?

Show solution

Because the token can move right but never left, its x-coordinate never decreases. Once it leaves a column (a vertical line x = c) by stepping right, it can never come back to that column. So the path can only ever revisit a point within a single column — among a run of vertical (up/down) moves at the same x.

Within a column, the token revisits a point exactly when it reverses vertical direction, and a reversal always appears as two consecutive moves UD or DU. So a path is self-avoiding if and only if its move-string over {U, D, R} never contains UD or DU.

Now just count length-N strings over {U, D, R} with no U next to a D. Grouping by the last move:

  • after R you may place R, U, or D;
  • after U you may place R or U;
  • after D you may place R or D.

Letting aN be the total count, this gives the recurrence

aN = 2·aN−1 + aN−2,   a1 = 3,   a2 = 7,

producing 3, 7, 17, 41, 99, 239, … — and in closed form

aN = ½ · [ (1+√2)N+1 + (1−√2)N+1 ].

These are the half-companion Pell numbers; the count grows by a factor of (1+√2) ≈ 2.414 per step.

2. The circle through the second-nearest point

Five points are dropped independently and uniformly at random inside a unit disk (radius 1, centred at O). Sort them by distance from the centre and draw the concentric circle that passes through the second-closest point. That circle contains exactly two of the five points — the closest one strictly inside, and the second-closest on its boundary.

What is the expected area of this circle? (Loosely: the smallest circle centred at O that captures two of the points.)

Show solution

The trick is to change variables. For a single uniform point in the unit disk, let R be its distance from the centre. The chance of landing within radius r is the area ratio r², so P(R ≤ r) = r² — which means R² is uniform on [0, 1].

The area of the concentric disk through a point at distance R is π R². So if we set U = R², then area ⁄ π = U is Uniform[0, 1] — independently for each of the five points.

We want the disk through the second-closest point, i.e. the second-smallest of the five U's. For n i.i.d. Uniform[0,1] values, the k-th smallest has expectation k ⁄ (n+1). With n = 5, k = 2:

E[U(2)] = 2 ⁄ 6 = 1 ⁄ 3.

Therefore the expected area is

E[π R(2)²] = π · E[U(2)] = π ⁄ 3 ≈ 1.047.
